If language can be said to have an underlying “substance,” as distinct from a support medium such as aurality or typographic visuality, then we should, I believe, give it the name of voice in a particular phenomenological sense. This is the voice that we read with and into. It is also the voice of style, what even conventional criticism calls the voice of the author. Voice in this sense is able to grasp différance. The constitutive differences of grammalepsis generate the voice of language.
